Clear answers, thoughtful choices

What whole-body donation means.

It is the donation of a body after death for education, training, or research. It is distinct from organ and tissue donation for transplant.

01

Registration is a first step—not a guarantee.

Programs have individual eligibility criteria and generally make a final decision at the time of death. Families should have an alternate plan.

02

Organ donation may still be possible.

Some programs can accept a donor after certain organs or tissues are donated, while others cannot. Confirm compatibility with both organizations.

03

Costs and transportation vary.

Some programs cover approved transportation and cremation; others may not. Ask what is included and whether geographic limits apply.

04

Timing and return of remains vary.

When cremated remains are returned, it may take weeks, months, or longer depending on the program and the nature of the contribution.

Policies differ by institution and can change. Always confirm eligibility, costs, timing, consent, transportation, and final disposition directly with the program you choose.

How donation works

A decision made with clarity.

Planning ahead gives you time to compare programs, document your wishes, and prepare the people closest to you.

View Colorado resources →

Choose a program

Research university and accredited non-transplant donation programs. Review their purpose, eligibility rules, geography, costs, and policies.

Register and provide consent

Complete the chosen program’s forms. Keep copies where family or the person responsible for your arrangements can find them.

Talk with the people you trust

Tell loved ones, healthcare agents, and care providers about your wishes. Give them the program’s 24-hour contact information.

When the time comes

The designated person contacts the program promptly. The program reviews eligibility and, if accepted, coordinates transfer according to its policy.

A contribution to medicine

The donation may serve education, clinical training, or research. Programs should treat every donor with dignity and professional care.

Final disposition

Many programs arrange cremation after study and may return cremated remains or offer another option. Exact timing and arrangements vary.

Why Gifts That Teach began

Curiosity became a calling.

Gifts That Teach was founded by Elle Franciscus after a high-school visit to a cadaver lab revealed the profound role donors play in medical learning. That experience strengthened her goal of becoming a physician and inspired her to help others understand whole-body donation.

I am a Senior at Vanderbilt University in Nashville studying biomedical engineering on the pre-med track and the Cadet Battalion Commander in the Army ROTC program. I am a student leader who is passionate about serving others in her community.

This project exists to make a complex and deeply personal decision feel more understandable—through respectful language, reliable starting points, and gratitude for the people who choose to teach beyond their lifetime.

Frequently asked

Questions worth asking.

The program you choose is the final authority on its policies and your eligibility.

Can my family still hold a memorial service?

Yes. Families can hold a memorial service before or during the donation period, usually without the body present. The timing and possibility of a later service with cremated remains depend on the program.

Does whole-body donation cost anything?

Some programs cover approved transportation, cremation, and return of cremated remains. Costs outside a service area, death certificates, memorials, or other arrangements may remain the family’s responsibility. Ask for details in writing.

Who is eligible to donate?

Eligibility differs. Age alone may not disqualify someone, but certain infectious diseases, trauma, autopsy, body size, organ recovery, or distance can affect acceptance. Final screening commonly occurs at death.

Can I donate organs and also donate my body?

Sometimes. Compatibility depends on which organs or tissues are recovered and the whole-body program’s criteria. Registering with both does not ensure both donations can occur, so speak with each organization.

Will my family learn how the donation was used?

Policies vary. Some programs provide general information or hold remembrance ceremonies, while others cannot share project details. Ask about communication and privacy before registering.

What should my family do at the time of death?

They should contact the chosen program immediately using its designated number. The program will review eligibility and explain next steps. Keep an alternate funeral plan because acceptance cannot always be guaranteed.
